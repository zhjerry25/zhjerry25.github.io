### 1.1 Introduction

Hydrogen
- Atomic number 1
- Atomic weight 1.008
- Electron configuration 1s<sup>1</sup>

Hydrogen is the lightest and most abundant chemical element in the universe, accounting for about 75% of all ordinary matter

The simplest atom: 1 proton + 1 electron

***
### 1.2 The H<sup>+</sup> and H<sup>-</sup> Ions

**The H<sup>+</sup> ion (proton)**

The ionization energy of a hydrogen atom is 1312 kJ·mol<sup>-1</sup>
Clearly, protons cannot exist under ordinary conditions. Therefore, H<sup>+</sup> in water exists mainly in forms such as $\mathrm{[H_3O]^+}$

Hydration of the proton is strongly exothermic   
$\Delta_{hyd} {H^o(H^+,g)} = -1091\ \mathrm{kJ·mol^{-1}}$

The $\mathrm{[H_3O]^+}$ ion is a well-defined species and has been crystallographically characterized in various salts (fig. 1/2)

![150](Hydroxonium-cation.svg)

![150](500px-Hydronium-3D-balls.webp)

The $\mathrm{[H_5O_2]^+}$ and $\mathrm{[H_9O_4]^+}$ ions have also been isolated in crystalline acid hydrates (fig. 3)

![200](500px-Zundel-cation.webp)

The $\mathrm{[H_5O_2]}$ and $\mathrm{[H_9O_4]^+}$ ions are also common members of the hydrated proton family. Hydrated protons are species of the form $\mathrm{[H(OH_2)_n]^+ \ (n=1-20)}$

<br>

**The H<sup>-</sup> ion**

The electron affinity $\Delta_{EA} \mathrm{H (298K)}$ of a hydrogen atom is $-73\ \mathrm{kJ·mol^{-1}}$

All alkali-metal hydrides have the NaCl crystal structure

Based on diffraction data and the radii of the metal ions, the radius of the H<sup>-</sup> ion ranges from 154 pm (LiH) to 133 pm (CsH), with an average value close to the 133 pm radius of F<sup>-</sup>

The atomic radius increases substantially from an H atom ( $r_\mathrm{{covalent}}= 37\ \mathrm{pm}$ ) to an H<sup>-</sup> ion. This is due to the interelectronic repulsion produced when the second electron enters the 1s atomic orbital

The relatively small hydride-ion radius in LiH gives the Li-H bond some covalent character. However, the calculated and experimental lattice energies of Group 1 metal hydrides show that the electrostatic model applies to every first-group metal hydride

The hydrides of s-block metals (except Be) can be prepared by heating H<sub>2</sub> with the metal

Ionic hydrides tend to decompose into their constituent elements. At the same time, salts of ionic hydrides with metals in high oxidation states are the least likely to exist

### 1.3 Isotopes of Hydrogen

**Deuterium**

The differences between H and D, or between H<sub>2</sub>O and D<sub>2</sub>O, arise from their different masses. These mass differences in turn affect their fundamental vibrational wavenumbers and zero-point energies (see the example problem)

The fundamental vibrational frequencies of H<sub>2</sub>, HD, and D<sub>2</sub> are 4159, 3630, and 2990 cm<sup>-1</sup>, respectively. The calculated zero-point energies of H<sub>2</sub> and D<sub>2</sub> are 26.0 and 18.4 kJ·mol<sup>-1</sup>, respectively

These molecules have identical total electronic binding energies, so their dissociation energies differ by $(26.0-18.4)=7.6\ \mathrm{kJ·mol^{-1}}$

The D-D bond is stronger than the H-H bond

![280](graph1.webp)

As shown in fig. 4, an R-D bond is also stronger than the corresponding R-H bond. This difference is the basis of the kinetic isotope effect

>Question 2: Kinetic Isotope Effect (KIE) and Zero-Point Vibrational Energy (ZPE)

**Calculating ZPE and KIE**  
The kinetic isotope effect (KIE) is the change in a reaction rate constant that occurs when an atom in a reactant molecule is replaced by one of its isotopes. The KIE can be used to identify the cleavage of a specific bond to hydrogen during a reaction. The harmonic oscillator model is commonly used to estimate the difference between the activation rates of C-H and C-D bonds.

The vibrational frequency ( $\mathrm{\nu}$ ) in the harmonic oscillator model can be expressed as:

$$
\nu = \frac{1}{2\pi} \sqrt{\frac{k}{\mu}} 
$$

Here, k is the force constant and $\mu$ is the reduced mass.

The vibrational energy of a molecule is:

$$
E_n = (n + \frac{1}{2})h \nu
$$ 

Here, n is the vibrational quantum number and can take the values 0, 1, 2, 3, ... The energy $E_n$ of the lowest vibrational energy level (n = 0) is called the zero-point vibrational energy (ZPE).

>2-A1) Calculate the reduced masses of C-H ( $\mu_{CH}$ ) and C-D ( $\mu_{CD}$ ) in atomic mass units, assuming that the mass of deuterium is twice that of hydrogen

**Solution**  
The reduced mass is the equivalent mass in a two-body problem and is calculated as

$$
\mu=\frac{m_1m_2}{m_1+m_2}
$$

For this problem

$$
\mu_{CH}=\frac{12.01\times1.008}{12.01+1.008}=0.9299\ \mathrm{amu}
$$

$$
\mu_{CD}=\frac{12.01\times2.016}{12.01+2.016}=1.726\ \mathrm{amu}
$$

Note that the unit is the atomic mass unit (amu)

>2-A2) Assuming that the force constants (k) of the C-H and C-D stretching vibrations are the same and that the C-H stretching frequency is 2900 $\mathrm{cm^{-1}}$, calculate
 the C-D stretching frequency (in $\mathrm{cm^{-1}}$)

**Solution**  
Substitute the values, taking care with the units

$$
\frac{\tilde{\nu}_1}{\tilde{\nu}_2}=\sqrt{\frac{\mu_2}{\mu_1}}
$$

Thus, $\tilde{\nu}_2= 2129\ \mathrm{cm^{-1}}$

>2-A3) Using the C-H and C-D stretching frequencies from 2-A2), calculate the zero-point energies (ZPE) of the C-H and C-D stretching vibrations
(in $\mathrm{kJ·mol^{-1}}$)

**Solution**  
Again, substitute the values, taking care with the units

$$
\begin{aligned}
E&=\frac{1}{2}h\nu=\frac{1}{2}h \times c\times N_A \times \tilde{\nu}
\\
\\
E_{\mathrm{CH}}&=\frac{1}{2} \times h \times c \times N_A \times 2900 \times 10^{2}= 17.35\ \mathrm{kJ\ mol^{-1}}
\\
\\
E_{\mathrm{CD}}&=\frac{1}{2} \times h \times c \times N_A \times 2128 \times 10^{2}= 12.73\ \mathrm{kJ\ mol^{-1}}
\end{aligned}
$$

<br>
Kinetic Isotope Effect (KIE)  
Because the zero-point vibrational energies differ, a compound and its corresponding deuterated compound can be expected to react at different rates.  
For C-H and C-D bond-cleavage reactions, their transition-state energies are identical, as are their product energies. Thus, the isotope effect depends on the difference between the ZPE values of C-H and C-D.

>2-A4) Calculate the difference between the bond dissociation energies (BDE) of the C-H and C-D bonds (BDECD - BDECH), in $\mathrm{kJ·mol^{-1}}$

**Solution**  
Simply subtract

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{BDE\ _{CD}-BDE\ _{CH}}&=\mathrm{ZPE\ _{CH}-ZPE\ _{CD}}
\\
\\
&=17.35-12.73\ \mathrm{kJ·mol^{-1}}
\\
\\
&=4.62\ \mathrm{kJ·mol^{-1}}
\end{aligned}
$$

>2-A5) Assuming that the activation energies ( $\mathrm{E_a}$ ) for C-H and C-D bond cleavage are approximately equal to their bond energies and that the Arrhenius pre-exponential factors for C-H and C-D bond cleavage are the same, calculate the ratio of their rate constants at 25 °C ( $k_\mathrm{{CH}}$ / $k_\mathrm{{CD}}$ )

**Solution**  
From the Arrhenius equation $k=Ae^{\frac{-E_a}{RT}}$

$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{k_{CH}}{k_{CD}}&=e^{-(ZPE\ _{CD}-ZPE\ _{CH})/RT}
\\
\\
&=e^{1.86}=6.45
\end{aligned}
$$


One more sign conversion is needed here: a lower ZPE means a higher activation energy

**Deuterated Compounds**

The chemical formula of heavy water is written as $\mathrm{D_2O}$

Compounds in which H atoms are replaced by D atoms have many uses, such as solvents for $\mathrm{^1H\ NMR}$ spectroscopy (think: why?)

Replacing H with D can significantly affect the properties of a compound
Their different boiling points indicate differences in intermolecular hydrogen bonding: hydrogen bonds in $\mathrm{D_2O}$ are stronger than those in $\mathrm{H_2O}$

An important industrial use of $\mathrm{D_2O}$ is as a moderator in nuclear reactors. D has a much lower neutron capture cross section than H, so $\mathrm{D_2O}$ can reduce the energy of fast neutrons produced by fission without appreciably reducing the neutron flux

Many fully or partially deuterated compounds are commercially available. The degree of deuterium labeling can be determined by mass spectrometry, density measurement (after conversion to water), or infrared spectroscopy

The separation of D from H in water is achieved by electrolysis. When an aqueous NaOH solution (at natural isotopic abundance) is electrolyzed using nickel electrodes, the separation factor is approximately 6. The choice of electrode is crucial to increasing this ratio

$$
\begin{aligned}
&\mathrm{Anode}: \mathrm{2H_2O} \longrightarrow \mathrm{O_2}+\mathrm{4H^+}+ \mathrm{4e^-}
\\
\\
&\mathrm{Cathode}: \mathrm{4H_2O}+\mathrm{4e^-} \longrightarrow \mathrm{2H_2}+ \mathrm{4[OH]^-}
\end{aligned}
$$

$$
s=\frac{\mathrm{(\frac{H}{D})_g}}{(\frac{H}{D})_l}
$$
